Personal reflection: As a career life sciences professional, a medical doctor, I have embraced AI - and continue to - as a tectonic shift. An idea whose time has come. From my vantage point in oncology and life sciences, it is delivering things that would have seemed impossible even five years ago. I am genuinely for it, and personally invested in it, especially when applied with rigor and intent.

But I am worried. And what worries me doesn't get covered adequately - certainly not on this platform, where there is too much investor excitement around big tech IPOs, enterprise partnerships with foundation model providers, and what not. Not enough coverage of the AI 'cave' where things are going sour. These should not be dismissed as 'transition costs' - a phrase that has become a rhetorical escape hatch, a way to acknowledge harm without taking responsibility for it.

***In that spirit:*** 

* **Employee morale in Big Tech:** take any - Amazon, Meta, others - is at an all time low. Layoffs that look arbitrary and excessive, putting the cart before the horse. Letting people go in anticipation of AI productivity gains that haven't yet materialised, and may not in the form assumed. Is it good management? Is it in good faith?
* **The agentic AI rush:** without 360 degree assessment - is where the most undisciplined behaviour lives. Automating because it is easy, not because it is better - with workflow implications poorly understood, token costs spiking by orders of magnitude, and value flowing overwhelmingly to model providers rather than the organisations deploying them.
* The **damage done to new graduates:** smart, hardworking, intelligent - who are left redundant even before they start careers, left with loans but few jobs out of university.  
* The **tech industry dominance over the choices and decisions:** with business leaders and governments - effectively becoming order takers - by choice, or by bedazzlement as tech gods come up with new ‘what AI can do for you’ tricks every 3 months. 

*We need balance. We need careful consideration. We need to put business first, not tech.* 

In oncology, we do not approve a therapy because it showed early promise and investors were excited. We run phased trials. We define outcome measures. We set harm thresholds before we scale. The rigour we apply to clinical evidence should be the baseline we demand from AI adoption

**If you are a business lead, three questions worth sitting with:**

1. D**o we have a full view of the total cost of AI enablement -** as you deploy agents to run loops (and runaway costs), outputs that need manual review at a velocity massively higher than traditional content development? In Pharma MLR teams will need to be size of sales force if all AI content generated for use has to be reviewed. I exaggerate to make the point! 
2. **How much true value output is being generated versus energetic and organizational waste?** If you are not tracking AI co-pilot usage, chat loops, and overall productivity systematically - you don't know and may be in for a surprise. Uber burned through its AI budget in four months. It won't be the last.
3. \[CEOs] **Can we demonstrate results** before letting go of people? 

 **And for the industry:**

* Can the **concentration of investment in a handful of model providers** racing toward trillion dollar IPOs be rebalanced toward applications that actually serve people?
* Can we stop the speculative rot and **invest in people and process before going all in** on technology?
* Can **organisations engage with AI at the pace they choose**, rather than have model companies pushing adoption on their terms - and their timelines?

Because if we don't course correct, **we are not looking at transition costs. We are looking at a significant pile-up.** And unlike in clinical medicine, there is no adverse event reporting system. No one is keeping score.
