## **The intelligence gap most organisations leave unused**

Every year, ASCO releases thousands of abstract titles weeks before the full abstract content becomes available. For most BD and early commercial teams, those weeks are dead time — a gap where the field sits and waits. The sprint begins only when the abstracts drop, by which point the lead time to prepare targeted engagement, schedule investigator conversations or initiate asset screening has already collapsed.

That gap is not dead time. It is intelligence time — if you have the tools to read it.

First-in-human studies represent the earliest public signal of a competitor's committed development investment. They are the moment a programme crosses from preclinical into the clinic and enters the competitive landscape formally. By the time teams begin traditional congress analysis, the window to position around these assets has already narrowed.

For ASCO 2026, Mito AI's VERISIGHT platform applied proprietary oncology entity recognition to abstract titles at point of release — identifying approximately 100 FIH assets across 15 tumour groups and 14 modality classes, structured and delivered within 48 hours of title publication, roughly three weeks before full abstracts became available on 21 May 2026.

## Five signals visible before a single abstract was read

### 01 · The ADC wave goes tumour-agnostic — at scale

Thirteen ADCs appear in the Pan Tumour category alone: ALK201, BG-C9074, DM002, DM005, DS3610, DXC006, GenSci139, SIM0505, SKB500, SYS6043, TAK-188, TGW101, TQB6411. This is not a coincidence of programme timing. It reflects a deliberate industry shift toward tumour-agnostic basket designs for ADC first-in-human studies, with indication-specific proof-of-concept to follow. The tumour-agnostic FIH design model is becoming the dominant paradigm for ADC programmes.

For BD teams, this changes the screening logic. A Pan Tumour FIH ADC is a platform bet on a target or a payload-linker combination, with indication optionality explicitly preserved. Assessing these assets requires reading the target biology before the tumour indication is narrowed.

### **02 · The PROTAC wave reaches clinical scale**

Six PROTACs and targeted protein degraders — DS9051, HRS-8080, HJ-004, PIN-5018, ST-01156, HRS-5041 — are entering the clinic across Prostate, Breast, Lung, Endocrine and Pan Tumour settings. This is a class-level transition, not a single programme milestone. Targeted protein degradation is no longer a frontier preclinical technology. It is a clinical modality with genuine pipeline breadth.

What makes this signal particularly legible from title data alone is that VERISIGHT identifies PROTAC nomenclature as a distinct modality class — something a keyword search or manual title review would collapse into the broader small molecule category. That distinction matters for BD teams assessing competitive moats, licensing opportunities and platform partnerships.

### **03 · Alpha-particle radiopharmaceuticals: the pipeline diversifies beyond lutetium-177**

Four alpha-emitting agents — \[212Pb]VMT01, 225Ac-MTI-201, \[225Ac]Ac-FL-020 and \[Ac-225]RTX-2358 — are entering the clinic across Melanoma, Prostate and Sarcoma. The radiopharmaceutical pipeline is actively diversifying into the alpha-particle space across multiple tumour settings simultaneously. For BD teams tracking radiopharmaceutical platform partnerships, targeting ligand assets and radioisotope supply chain positions, this FIH wave is a direct signal of where the next cycle of licensing and acquisition activity is likely to concentrate.
