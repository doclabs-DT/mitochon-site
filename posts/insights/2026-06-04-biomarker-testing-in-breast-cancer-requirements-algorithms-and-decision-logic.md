## Executive Summary

* **Upfront tissue IHC for ER, PR, and HER2 is universally mandatory** at diagnosis regardless of stage; beyond this shared foundation, the testing pathway bifurcates sharply - early-stage disease routes toward recurrence risk stratification (Ki-67, multigene panels), while metastatic disease triggers comprehensive somatic NGS, tumor-agnostic panel testing, and broad germline evaluation simultaneously.
* **Genomic biomarkers now govern treatment eligibility across all metastatic lines**: PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN status gates PI3K/AKT pathway inhibitors; ESR1 mutation detected by ctDNA triggers oral SERD eligibility; germline BRCA1/2 and PALB2 pathogenic variants unlock PARP inhibition - each with companion diagnostics that are regulatory-approved and non-interchangeable.
* **Reflex testing rules are as clinically critical as initial testing**: six defined triggers - HER2 IHC 2+ equivocal, local HER2 IHC 0, metastatic recurrence, rapid CDK4/6i progression, T-DXd progression, and ctDNA negative with low tumor fraction - each mandate a specific downstream test, with failure to reflex directly translating to missed therapeutic eligibility.
* **Real-world testing adoption is alarmingly low**: despite mandatory guideline requirements, only 25% of patients in the DREAM EU study received biomarker testing for second-line therapy routing in HR+/HER2- metastatic disease - a gap that represents direct, measurable harm to patients who would otherwise qualify for targeted therapies.
* **The HER2 classification system has materially expanded** with the addition of HER2-low (IHC 1+ or IHC 2+/ISH-) and HER2-ultralow (faint IHC 0 in ≤10% of cells) categories, both now conferring T-DXd eligibility; approximately 64% of locally scored HER2-negative tumors are reclassified as low or ultralow on central reassessment, making local IHC 0 a mandatory reflex trigger rather than a terminal result.

## Strategic context - why it matters

Biomarker testing is no longer a supporting function of breast cancer care - it is the mechanism by which patients are matched to therapies that can materially extend their lives. The expansion of the targetable landscape over the past five years has been extraordinary: where once a single IHC panel at diagnosis was sufficient to guide systemic therapy for years, the field now requires serial, molecularly guided reassessment at every major disease transition point. This shift has profound implications for how oncology teams, pathology services, and payers think about testing infrastructure.

The commercial and clinical stakes are high on both sides. For patients, incomplete or delayed biomarker testing means therapies with approved indications - inavolisib for PIK3CA-mutated early relapsers, elacestrant for ESR1-mutated post-CDK4/6i progressors, T-DXd for HER2-low and ultralow disease - go unidentified and unprescribed. The DREAM EU real-world data make this concrete: only 25% of eligible HR+/HER2- metastatic patients received second-line biomarker testing in routine practice. The other 75% were making treatment decisions without the molecular information that guidelines require.

For pharmaceutical companies and access teams, the implication is symmetrical: the commercial reach of molecularly targeted therapies is bounded not by clinical efficacy or formulary placement alone, but by the testing infrastructure that identifies eligible patients. A therapy with a 35% biomarker prevalence - roughly the PIK3CA mutation rate in HR+/HER2- metastatic disease - is functionally a therapy with 8-9% real-world reach if testing rates sit at 25%. Closing the testing gap is therefore not a compliance exercise; it is the rate-limiting step for realising the therapeutic value of the entire targeted oncology pipeline in breast cancer.

The complexity of co-dependent eligibility rules adds a further layer. Most targeted agents in this space require not one biomarker but two or three to be confirmed simultaneously - receptor status, genomic alteration, and clinical context. Systems that test for PIK3CA mutation without confirming HER2-negative status produce unactionable results. Systems that detect ESR1 mutation at baseline rather than at progression conflate a prognostic finding with a predictive one. Getting the testing architecture right - what to test, when, in which specimen type, and with which reflex rules embedded - is as clinically important as knowing which therapy to prescribe.

The three decision frameworks that follow - upfront testing sequence, reflex rules, and serial longitudinal logic - collectively define the minimum viable testing programme for HR+/HER2- breast cancer in 2026. They are not aspirational standards; they are the operational backbone of guideline-concordant care.

## Biomarker testing requirements - what must be tested and when

Biomarker testing in breast cancer is not a single event - it is a longitudinal programme that begins at diagnosis, evolves at recurrence, and adapts dynamically at each line of therapy progression.

At initial diagnosis, ER, PR, and HER2 status by IHC and ISH are universally required regardless of stage (NCCN 2A; ESMO I, B). These results classify phenotype, gate systemic therapy selection, and anchor all subsequent testing decisions. Ki-67 by IHC is recommended (ESMO I, A) for proliferation assessment, with the abemaciclib Ki-67 IHC MIB-1 pharmDx companion diagnostic providing the regulatory basis for adjuvant CDK4/6i eligibility when Ki-67 is ≥20% alongside node-positive high-risk features.

For early-stage HR+/HER2- disease specifically, recurrence risk stratification by RNA-based multigene expression assays - Oncotype DX (recurrence score ≥26 for high risk), MammaPrint, Prosigna (PAM50), or EndoPredict - is guideline-recommended (NCCN 1; ESMO II, B) in node-negative or N1 populations to guide chemotherapy omission decisions. Critically, an Oncotype RS of 16-25 in younger premenopausal node-negative patients still requires chemotherapy, a nuance that demands the treating physician review the full clinical context rather than apply a single threshold.

In the metastatic setting, the testing requirement broadens substantially. Comprehensive multigene panel testing (MGPT) is mandated at metastatic confirmation and should encompass somatic NGS for PIK3CA, AKT1, and ESR1 (minimum ≥0.1% VAF threshold for ctDNA); tumor-agnostic markers including TMB (≥10 mut/Mb), MSI-H/dMMR, NTRK1/2/3 fusions, and RET fusions; and full germline evaluation for BRCA1/2 and PALB2. Each of these carries distinct therapeutic implications and approved companion diagnostics.

For TNBC, PD-L1 status by IHC is required (NCCN 1; ESMO I, A) using the 22C3 assay (CPS ≥10) for pembrolizumab eligibility, or SP142 (IC ≥1%) for atezolizumab in jurisdictions where that approval is maintained (EMA only - the FDA indication was withdrawn). These two antibody clones are not interchangeable and evaluate different scoring systems; substituting one for the other invalidates the result for regulatory purposes.

## Co-dependent eligibility - why single-biomarker thinking fails

A critical and frequently underappreciated feature of breast cancer biomarker logic is co-dependency - most targeted therapies require confirmation of multiple biomarkers simultaneously, not just one.

Capivasertib and inavolisib both require documented ER+/HER2- receptor status alongside a PIK3CA, AKT1, or PTEN pathway alteration. ESR1-targeted therapies (elacestrant, camizestrant) require HR+/HER2- status and ESR1 mutation detected specifically after progression on prior endocrine therapy - ESR1 testing performed before endocrine treatment has no eligibility relevance. Adjuvant abemaciclib requires HR+/HER2- status, Ki-67 ≥20%, and node-positive or high-grade disease features. Pembrolizumab in breast cancer requires confirmed triple-negative status - PD-L1 CPS ≥10 alone is insufficient without ER-/PR-/HER2- confirmation. Adjuvant olaparib requires germline BRCA1/2 mutation plus high-risk residual disease features post-neoadjuvant therapy.

The practical implication is that incomplete testing - testing one biomarker but not the co-requisite - produces an unactionable result. A patient who is ESR1-positive by ctDNA but untested for ER/HER2 receptor status cannot be confirmed eligible for elacestrant. A patient with PIK3CA mutation confirmed but HER2 status unverified cannot be routed to inavolisib. Testing programmes must be designed with co-dependency in mind, not as individual biomarker silos.

A further complexity is the PTEN NGS versus IHC discordance: approximately 5.4% of tumors classified as wild-type for PTEN, PIK3CA, and AKT1 by NGS demonstrate PTEN protein loss by IHC, meaning NGS-negative patients are not necessarily pathway-inhibitor ineligible. Multimodal testing is required to capture the full eligible population.

## Reflex rules - the six mandatory triggers

Reflex testing - the automatic initiation of a downstream test based on a specific result or clinical event - is as important as initial testing and considerably less consistently followed.

**HER2 IHC 2+ equivocal** mandates reflex to FISH/ISH to confirm or exclude gene amplification. This is the foundational reflex rule and is followed with reasonable consistency.

**Local HER2 IHC 0** now mandates reflex to central 4B5 assay reassessment. The rationale is compelling: approximately 64% of locally scored IHC 0 tumors are reclassified as HER2-low or HER2-ultralow on central reassessment, and both categories now confer T-DXd eligibility. Local IHC 0 is no longer a terminal negative result - it is a trigger.

**First metastatic recurrence** mandates repeat tissue biopsy with full ER/PR/HER2 re-evaluation. Approximately 23.4% of patients experience phenotypic switch at recurrence - a rate high enough that treating based on primary tumor receptor status alone will mis-stratify roughly one in four patients. The metastatic biopsy is not optional documentation; it is a therapeutic decision point.

**Rapid progression (within 6 months) on first-line CDK4/6i** mandates reflex to ESR1 and PIK3CA genotyping to expedite sequencing decisions toward elacestrant or ADCs rather than continuing sequential endocrine therapy.

**Progression on T-DXd** mandates HER2 reassessment. Complete HER2 loss is observed in approximately 52% of patients progressing on T-DXd - a rate that fundamentally changes the subsequent therapeutic landscape and invalidates prior HER2 scoring for future ADC routing decisions.

**ctDNA negative for PIK3CA/ESR1 with low tumor fraction** mandates reflex to tissue-based NGS. Diagnostic reliability of plasma NGS drops sharply below 0.1% VAF, and a negative ctDNA result in a low-tumor-fraction sample is not a true negative - it is an uninformative result that requires tissue confirmation before excluding pathway inhibitor eligibility.

## Serial and longitudinal testing - tracking acquired resistance

The dynamic nature of HR+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer - with resistance emerging at every line of therapy - means that biomarker testing must be serialised, not performed once at metastatic diagnosis and then referenced indefinitely.

The longitudinal testing framework has two main branches that activate at different events. At first metastatic recurrence, the tissue branch activates: repeat biopsy with ER, PR, and HER2 re-evaluation to capture phenotypic switching (inter-site receptor discordance is reported at 15-40% between primary and metastatic sites). Upon progression on endocrine therapy, the liquid biopsy branch activates: ctDNA for acquired resistance mutations. High concordance exists between plasma ctDNA and tissue NGS for targeted alterations - FoundationOne Liquid CDx demonstrates 88.6% overall percent agreement with tissue FoundationOne CDx for PIK3CA alterations, and the Cobas PCR test achieves 94.0% concordance with tissue NGS.

ESR1 mutation detection by ctDNA is the paradigm case for liquid biopsy-guided therapy adaptation. ESR1 mutations emerge under the selective pressure of aromatase inhibitor therapy and are almost exclusively detected in the metastatic setting after endocrine progression. Their detection at the point of progression gates eligibility for oral SERDs including elacestrant, and - following SERENA-6 - the window for detection has now expanded to include ctDNA-emerging ESR1 mutation even before radiographic progression, making serial surveillance a therapeutic opportunity rather than merely a confirmatory test.

When ctDNA is negative with low tumor fraction, the result is technically uninformative and mandates reflex to tissue NGS rather than interpretation as biomarker-negative. This is a common failure point in real-world practice.

Emerging MRD and ctDNA surveillance approaches - tumor-informed tracking assays such as RaDaR and Signatera deployed in studies like SURVIVE - are beginning to demonstrate the ability to detect molecular relapse before clinical evidence of disease. However, direct clinical decision-making based on ctDNA MRD positivity in the adjuvant setting remains investigational outside of trials.

## Emerging biomarkers and evolving thresholds

Several biomarker definitions are actively in flux, and clinicians applying static thresholds risk misclassifying patients as ineligible for therapies for which they would qualify under current criteria.

The HER2 classification system has undergone the most consequential expansion. The binary HER2-positive/negative framework has been replaced by a four-tier system: HER2-positive (IHC 3+ or ISH-amplified), HER2-low (IHC 1+ or IHC 2+/ISH-), HER2-ultralow (faint IHC 0 in ≤10% of cells), and HER2-null (IHC 0 with no staining). T-DXd is now approved across the low and ultralow categories, making accurate pathological distinction between true IHC 0 and faint IHC 0 a direct determinant of therapy access.

Ki-67 thresholds are evolving toward a functional endpoint. The traditional ≥20% proliferation cutoff for abemaciclib eligibility is being complemented by a stricter Complete Cell Cycle Arrest (CCCA) definition - post-treatment Ki-67 ≤2.7% following preoperative endocrine therapy - which better captures the functional response that predicts long-term benefit.

HR-low tumors (ER/PR expression 1-9%) are increasingly recognised as a distinct subgroup that behaves phenotypically more like TNBC than conventional HR+ disease. These patients are being enrolled in TNBC systemic trial algorithms, and the binary HR+/HR- classification is being replaced by a three-tier framework that accommodates HR-low as a separate treatment category.

UGT1A1 pharmacogenomics - specifically the UGT1A1*28/*28 homozygous genotype - is emerging as a clinically important pharmacogenomic marker for sacituzumab govitecan, conferring substantially elevated risk of severe neutropenia and diarrhea. While not yet a standard upfront test, dose-modification algorithms based on UGT1A1 genotyping are under active investigation and are likely to become incorporated into prescribing practice.

AI and digital pathology tools are beginning to intersect with biomarker testing. H&E-based AI models (including Ataraxis Breast and AI-PathClinRS) show independent prognostic value, and high digital TIL expression (≥0.3) enriches for TP53 mutations and predicts greater magnitude of abemaciclib benefit in the monarchE subgroup analysis - though this remains hypothesis-generating and should not yet modify patient selection outside of trials.

The systemic inflammatory marker NLR (neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio ≥2.3) is an independent risk predictor for T-DXd-related interstitial lung disease, a safety-relevant application that may influence monitoring intensity rather than treatment selection.

## The testing gap - from guideline mandate to clinical reality

Despite the breadth and clinical importance of the biomarker testing framework described above, real-world uptake remains profoundly inadequate. The DREAM EU study found only a 25% utilisation rate of biomarker testing for second-line therapy routing in HR+/HER2- metastatic disease - meaning three quarters of patients eligible for molecularly guided therapy are not receiving the test that would identify their eligibility.

This is not an abstract quality metric. In a disease where PIK3CA mutation rates are approximately 35-40%, ESR1 mutations emerge in 30-40% of patients after aromatase inhibitor therapy, and germline BRCA1/2 pathogenic variants are present in 5-10%, the gap between testing mandates and real-world practice translates directly into patients not receiving approved therapies for which they qualify.

Strategies to close this gap - reflex testing protocols embedded in laboratory workflows, automated MGPT ordering triggered by metastatic confirmation, and molecular tumour board review at each progression event - are now as important a clinical priority as the identification of new biomarkers.
